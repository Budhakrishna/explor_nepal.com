Geography

Lumbini Province is located in the southern part of Nepal and is named after Lumbini, the birthplace of Lord Buddha. It covers an area of 22,288 km² and is bordered by India to the south, Gandaki Province to the north, and Sudurpashchim Province to the west. The province is known for its fertile plains, religious significance, and cultural heritage.

Districts

Lumbini Province is divided into 12 districts, each with its unique cultural and geographical significance:

  • Rupandehi: Home to Lumbini, the birthplace of Lord Buddha.
  • Kapilvastu: Known for its historical and archaeological sites.
  • Nawalparasi (East): A district with a mix of hills and plains.
  • Palpa: Known for its cultural heritage and scenic beauty.
  • Arghakhanchi: Famous for its natural beauty and religious sites.
  • Gulmi: Known for its terraced fields and cultural festivals.
  • Rukum (East): A district with stunning landscapes and cultural diversity.
  • Pyuthan: Known for its historical significance and natural beauty.
  • Dang: A district with a mix of hills and plains.
  • Banke: Known for its religious sites and natural beauty.
  • Bardiya: Home to the Bardiya National Park.
  • Rolpa: Known for its cultural heritage and historical significance.

Culture, Castes, and Religions

Lumbini Province is a diverse region with a rich cultural heritage. The major ethnic groups include the Tharu, Brahmin, Chhetri, Magar, and Newar communities. Each group has its unique traditions, festivals, and religious practices:

  • Tharu: Indigenous communities known for their traditional dances like Sakhiya and Jhumara.
  • Brahmin and Chhetri: Follow Hindu traditions and celebrate festivals like Dashain and Tihar.
  • Magar: Known for their cultural heritage and festivals like Maghe Sankranti.
  • Newar: Known for their intricate art and architecture, and festivals like Indra Jatra.
